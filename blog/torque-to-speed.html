From Torque to Speed

Modeling a Self-Balancing Robot

One of the key challenges in controlling a self-balancing robot is understanding how the control input — motor torque — translates into motion. While many implementations treat velocity as the input, the physical system is fundamentally driven by torque. This post derives the relationship between torque and angular velocity, and discusses how it can be obtained analytically and through system identification.

1. Problem Statement

For a two-wheeled balancing robot, the motors apply torque to the wheels. However, most control strategies operate in terms of angular velocity or linear velocity. This raises an important question:

How do we go from torque to angular speed?

2. Physical Intuition

Torque does not directly produce velocity — it produces angular acceleration. The relationship is governed by Newton’s second law for rotation:

τ = J · α

where τ is torque, J is the moment of inertia, and α is angular acceleration. To obtain angular velocity, we integrate:

ω = ∫ α dt

This already suggests that the system has inherent dynamics — it is not an instantaneous mapping.

3. Equivalent Inertia (Jeq)

In a self-balancing robot, the motor does not only accelerate the wheel — it also affects the body of the robot. Therefore, we define an equivalent inertia:

Jeq = Jw + mbl²

where:
Jw = wheel inertia
mb = body mass
l = distance from wheel axis to center of mass

This approximation captures the coupling between translational and rotational dynamics.

4. From Torque to Angular Velocity

Substituting the equivalent inertia into the dynamics:

τ = Jeq · α

gives:

α = τ / Jeq

Integrating:

ω(t) = ∫ (τ / Jeq) dt

In discrete-time control systems, this becomes:

ω[k+1] = ω[k] + (τ[k] / Jeq) · Δt

This is the fundamental relationship used when converting torque commands into velocity updates in simulation or control.

5. Practical Reality: Unknown Dynamics

In practice, Jeq is rarely known accurately. Additional effects include:

  • Motor dynamics
  • Friction and slip
  • Back EMF
  • Load disturbances

Because of this, a purely analytical model often deviates from reality.

6. System Identification Approach

Instead of deriving the full physics, we can experimentally determine the relationship between torque and velocity.

A simple approach:

  • Apply known torque inputs
  • Measure resulting angular velocity
  • Fit a dynamic model

This often results in a first-order system:

ω(s) / τ(s) = K / (Js + b)

where b represents damping and K captures motor constants.

7. Key Insight

The most important takeaway is this:

Torque controls acceleration, not velocity.

Any controller that outputs velocity is implicitly relying on an internal model — whether explicit or learned — that converts that velocity into torque.

Understanding this relationship is crucial when designing controllers such as PID, LQR, or Model Predictive Control, especially on embedded systems where computational and sensing resources are limited.
